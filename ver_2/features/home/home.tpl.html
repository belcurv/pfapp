What is pfapp?

pfapp (Personal Finance App) is a small collection of utilities for calculations related to personal finance. Everybody can use it! If you are unfamiliar with some of the theory or concepts, click on the little help icons.

FIRE Calculator

Calulations related to Financial Independence / Early Retirement.

Commute Calculator

How much is your commute costing you?

Portfolio

A (very) simple tracker, designed to calculate the aggregate returns of a collection of stock and bond mutual funds. Also includes portfolio reallocation suggestion based on the target returns calculated in the FIRE Calculator.

History

Before there was pfapp there was a simple spreadsheet calculator using a variety of TVM functions (Time Value of Money) to calculate the future value and required returns to achieve a hypothetical savings target. The spreadsheet originally just compared a few college savings strategies (savings rates and returns over time). At the time, we were awaiting the birth of our first child and I was planning for her eventual higher education expenses.

As the spreadsheet evolved, the college-savings features were removed and replaced by more retirement-appropriate features. I added a Net Worth calculator, which just summed columns of manually-input monthly data. Then I added a Portfolio worksheet that calculated the allocation ratio of a portfolio of stocks and bonds as well as its theoretical future value, based on manually-input data. Finally, I added a FIRE calculator worksheet which used results from the other worksheets to compare my hypothetical goals to the reality of my account balances and historical returns.

I always had the goal of making the spreadsheet an actual web application, but I did not possess the javascript experience to do so until mid-2016.

TVM Functions

TVM functions are built into nearly every spreadsheet (Excel, Google Sheets, LibreOffice Calc, etc) and are very easy to use. There are five basic component parts to all of the TVM functions:

  • Pmt: the recurring payment, whether you're using it as savings or paying off some debt.
  • Rate: the Interest rate for the loan or earned by the savings/investment.
  • Nper: the total number of payments for the loan or number of contributions to a savings/investment account. For a target savings calculation like my college savings example, you can think of this like the number of periods before you need the savings.
  • Pv: The present value of a debt or savings/investment.
  • Fv: The future value, or a cash balance you want to attain after the last payment is made.

Accordingly there are five TVM functions, each solving for one of the above five components, given the other components (italicized components are optional):

  • NPer(rate, pmt, pv, fv, type) solves for Number of Periods
  • Rate(nper, pmt, pv, fv, type, guess) solves for periodic interest rate
  • PV(rate, nper, pmt, fv, type) solves for present value
  • PMT(rate, nper, pv, fv, type) solves for annuity payment
  • FV(rate, nper, pmt, pv, type) solves for future value

The math behind all of the above (except Rate) is basic algebra. For example, the Future Value function is a geometric series:
FV = pv * rate^n + pmt (rate^n - 1)/(rate-1)

But Rate is a different beast. The Rate function is an open problem solvable only though iteration (aka: convergence, approximation). Rate works by iterating from a starting guess (thus the optional guess component), finding successively better approximate solutions. It will either converge on a good enough solution or not find one at all. My Rate calculator uses Newton's Method.

Privacy

Neither this web site (belcurv.com) nor pfapp use cookies, nor do they track you or collect any personal information from you. Features do make use of HTML5 web storage - a method of storing a limited amount of data in your browser's own local storage. Again, none of this information is transmitted to the internet or otherwise collected.
